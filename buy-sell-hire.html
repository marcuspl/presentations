Accidental Complexity

Key concept to understand why software development is hard
"Everything should be made as simple as possible, but not simpler"
As a project grows, the requirements change. A simple analogy might be a company. The amount of "stuff" needed to deal with itself is very different between a one-man startup and big company with hundreds of employees.
The accumulated accidental complexity of a project is called technical debt.
The correct level of abstraction or implementation is a moving target and will change with:
  • the size of the code base
  • the competence and size of the team
  • quality requirements of the code base
We are basically always choosing between:
  • accumulating technical debt in the form of
    • accidental complexity (things got more complicated than they should be)
    • shortcuts (not complex enough but good for now)
  • Re-writing old code to an adequate level of complexity
This is important to know when starting with technological development because either:
  1. Slower start but last longer (spend time estimating the complexity of the problem, choosing tools etc.)
  2. Fast start but risk of productivity stagnation
For an MVP or even for PMF-level product, the latter might be the right choice. Something always trumps nothing.
This concept applies also to the aspiring entrepreneur, because so much of what is required when starting a company is accidental complexity compared to the original value proposition.

Uncertainty and estimation

Software estimation is considered a black art.
Notoriously hard.
We always get it wrong. Even when we know we always get it wrong.
By the templates of the construction industry: always a no-go.
How hard can it be? Attempt #1: Novice.
  1. Write a spec.
  2. Ask a programmer how long he needs to build it?

This approach has been proven to always be wrong.

Attempt #2: Project manager.
  1. Write a spec.
  2. Ask a programmer how long he needs to build it?
  3. Multiply programmer's answers with 2.

Better but mostly wrong.

Attempt #3: Experienced project manager.
  1. Write a spec.
  2. Ask a programmer how long he needs to build it?
  3. Multiply programmer's answers with PI.

Getting closer.

Attempt #4: Industrial company attempt to normalize it.
  1. Write a spec.
  2. Ask a programmer how long he needs to build it?
  3. Ask programmer's colleague how long he needs to build it.
  4. Ask programmer's boss how long he needs to build it.
  5. Ask each person to give a pessimistic, an optimistic and a reasonable option.
  6. Take the avarage of the pessimistic answers.

It will still be wrong but it will be better.

All of this is also assuming that the specs are near perfect. Which they are not.
Which means; even as a non-technical founder, A LOT more is in your hands than you probably think.

Co-founder

  1. Understand what you're trying to do better than you do
  2. Is better able to communicate your thoughts than you are
  3. Specializes in the areas you suck at
  4. Makes all others seem a bit pale and uninterresting
  5. Raises the ability but even more importantly the potential of the company
  6. Just gets it
  7. Pretty much like finding a wife or husband
  1. Are hard to come by
  2. Don't be stingy - try to work out how you can do this together
  3. Have commitment plans that go deeper that vesting agreements
  4. Help each other maintain good spiritual health
  5. Always do your best to make sure that the other can proceed without you (serve)
  6. Please make room for a period of engagement
It's a bit like the monks say about a contrite heart: (roughly)

You either have one or you don't. If you don't have one, there is no use preaching about it.

  • If you find or have one - what can you as a non-tech do?
  • "Get them coffee and cheese burgers." -- Paul Graham
  • (Please do listen to the full How To Start A Startup-podcast)

Partner

  • the first lead-developer that has prooven him/herself
  • can work with you / the team
  • has proven to make write code that runs
  • said code has been externally reviewed and said not to be crap
  • has proven to not require almost any management at all (except for the standard project management stuff that all of us could use)
  • has leadership / management potential
  • is able to make progress without project management
  • demonstrate ability to be both sloppy and thorough

Hire

  • Wider range of competence and commitment
  • A free junior vs and expensive senior?
  • Cost of management?
  • Cost of communication?
  • Inexperienced enthusiasm trumps experienced bitterness.
Inhouse (co-founder, partner, hire) evaluation challenges and notes:
  • Perserverance maybe the most important trait, and the hardest to evaluate.
  • Note: many programmers look seem a bit pist-off when working hard.
  • Technology and language choices are not important unless super-junior.
  • Beware of gap between willingness and ability.
Inhouse (co-founder, partner, hire) conclusions
  • Co-founder is prefered but rare
  • Partners are found among hires
  • Inhouse is prefered over external due to
  • Cost of maintining understanding of "trivial complexity"
  • Cost of picking up an abandoned project
  • Requires and allows for non-developers to take ownership of YOUR OWN project

Web shop / Consultants

  • Look for qualified project management
  • Look through portfolio
  • Ask to meet the team
  • Discuss procedures for end-of-project uncertainty
  • Ask to be included and guided on the inside, to learn
A good option when/if:
  • You can afford it AND
  • Lack competence or willingness to
    • hire
    • specify project
    • manage project
A good option when/if:
  • Your project is well defined and understood
  • The shop has extensive experience of how to do that
  • Could potentially re-use portolio project (but don't count on it)
Drawbacks include:
  • Expensive
  • Out of Money?
  • Painful End-of-project conflicts and lock-ins
  • Lack of build-up of in-house competence
  • Have to restart the hiring project at a later stage (if you're building a company)

Upwork and Hubstaff

A good option when/if:
  • Well defined project
  • Willing and able to do project management
  • Comfortable navigating cultural differences
  • Willing to spend time finding the right match
Beware of challenges:
  • Timezones and Cultural distance
  • Agenices vs free lancers
  • Non-tech or inexperienced project managers may have a hard time
  • Price may or may not be an indicator of quality
  • $20 / h a decent indicator
However, it might be worth it: bubb.la and cor.ax

Learn It Yourself

For most it is not going to be the best investment of your time. But for some!
  • Beginners enthusiasm goes a long way
  • Not knowing the correct way can sometimes yield new ways
  • If you have more time than money
  • Or sick of waiting for the right person for the job

Conclusions and recommendations

Even more opinonated

Progress trumps perfection - go with what you can go with

If you can, go with any inhouse option

  • If you're in this for a few years, keep it as closely to you as possible
  • Allows you to step into
    • project management
    • specifications
    • content management
    • prioritization
  • Even if it means you'll have to learn some new things
The risk of not doing things in-house:
  • Wishful thinking that someone else will solve your problems
  • Lack of ownership of the development process
  • Lack of buildup of technical experties
  • Lack of buildup of knowledge about your own product
The risk of not doing things in-house:
  • The project becoming abandoned
  • Potential lack of hand-over between teams/developers
The benefits of not doing things inhouse
  • Something else is your bottleneck (highly unlikely in the early stages)
  • Inability or unwillingness to partake of the process
  • A trivial enough product that is done when it is done and you don't have to think about it
The benefits of not doing things inhouse
  • Potentially getting a high-performance team
  • Getting very well-qualified project management
  • You could find your perfect match!
Which one is for me?
  • More time than money? All inhouse options and Upwork.
  • More money than time? Consultant shop.
  • Some money and some time? Consider Upwork. Definitely try to hire.
  • Always look for the next perfect hire/partner.
  • Always learn more how to manage your project/product.
  • Are you building a company or a product?
Conclusions revisited: If you are building a company - inhouse is recommended.

Github

  • Get on there. Not an option.
  • Take ownership of the development of your product.
  • Minimize boilerplate when onboarding.
  • Makes you less sensitive to teams or developers leaving.
  • Excellent for content production.
  • Allows for manic founders to control product details.

Github

  • Has wiki for documentation.
  • Has "issues" that can be used as a ticket-system.
  • "Natively" supports trying out new stuff and pivoting with so called branches.
  • Keeps track of developer productivity.
  • Good place to collect specifications and design as well.

Github

  • Bonus: Github pages!
  • Check out my dummy at: https://marcuspl.github.io/product-dummy-dummy/
  • More on developing prototypes this way below.
  • Host your company website for free!
  • Check out my collague: http://stephenhuey.com/

Know what you want

  • A LOT of software development is about specifications.
  • Specifications is partly about knowing what you want.
  • Start specifying your product in any way you can.
  • Be creative!
  • (Almost) NOTHING in your product is going to show up without you intending it.
  • More below.

User Stories

  • Write down all the things the user is going to be able to do as stories.
  • User logs in. Sees list of things. Clicks on first thing. Logs out. etc.
  • Google "user stories" but don't get caught up in format or formalities.
  • Basically, if you give the stories to a developer, your product will be build. That's the goal.
  • VERY powerful tool.
  • Upload them to github to track changes. ;)

Produce Content

  • Developers often use placeholder text: Lorem Ipsum.
  • The content is part of your product.
  • Get help from copy-writers.

Design

  • Design doesn't have to be nice (but can be!)
  • Get your pens and papers out and draw the different views of the product
  • ... and their different states.
  • Get a designer or do it yourself!
  • A lot of our early pitches was showing a pdf that the designer made!

Excel and data modeling

  • If your product contains different states of data - mock it in excel or similar
  • Even if the data is too advanced, try to write down what you believe it will be
  • Workflow based on states of data could and should be easily modelled in Excel.

Dummy Prototypes

  • Check out Product-dummy-dummy
  • Take subset of html (that you'll learn in 15 minutes) and start building your prototype.
  • Skip all logic (login forms just doesn nothing) and mock all data.
  • Try to cover your full product.
  • Make a site-map of it.

UI Design Tools

  • I love Ionic but haven't yet tried their App Creator
  • Google "user interface sketch" "ui mockups" etc.
